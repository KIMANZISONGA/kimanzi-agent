Een beetje Nederlands gaat ver

Dutch professionals will not expect you to speak Dutch. But if you open with one word or phrase in their language, the effect is immediate — it breaks the ice, it shows effort, and it makes them smile. You don't need to say much. Just enough.

Here are ten phrases worth knowing. Listen to them, practise the pronunciation, and use them when the moment feels right. Don't force it — but don't be afraid to try either.

The phrases
Dutch Sounds like Meaning & when to use
Welkom VEL-kom Welcome — say it at the airport when you first meet them
Goedemorgen WHO-duh-MOR-gen Good morning — works perfectly when you meet them the next day
Hoe gaat het? Who HAHT het How are you? — a natural opener, casual and friendly
Geen probleem Hkane pro-BLAME No problem — use when the client apologises for something or asks a small favour
Alsjeblieft ALS-yuh-bleeft Please / here you go — say it when handing something over
Dank je wel DANK yuh vel Thank you — warm and informal, used constantly in Dutch culture
Tot ziens Tot ZEENS Goodbye / see you — say it at the end of an assignment
Gezellig Hkuh-ZEL-likh Cosy / fun / good vibes — a cultural favourite with no direct English translation. Use when you're at a nice spot together.
Lekker LEK-ker Tasty / nice / great — Dutch use this for food, weather, situations. If the food is good, say it.
Doe maar gewoon Doo mar hkuh-WOON "Just be normal" — a Dutch saying that captures their whole culture. Don't show off, don't be overly formal. Just be yourself.

A word on "Doe maar gewoon"

This phrase — literally "just act normal" — is one of the most Dutch things you can know. It reflects a cultural value: don't be flashy, don't overdo it, don't try too hard. Just show up as yourself and do good work. Funnily enough, that is also the best description of what a great KIMANZI host does.

Section 1

What KIMANZI is — and isn't

KIMANZI sits in a specific position. We are not a tour guide service. We are not a luxury concierge. We are the professional ground support that Dutch expats and business travellers need during their first days in Nairobi — and sometimes beyond.

Our clients arrive with a job to do. They are horticulture consultants, NGO project managers, fintech developers, and logistics coordinators. Nairobi is not their holiday — it is their workplace. They need someone who helps them land smoothly so they can focus on their work.

The gap we fill

Between a generic city tour and a full-service luxury handler, there is a space for something more human and more practical. That is where KIMANZI operates. Think of yourself as a knowledgeable, reliable local professional — not a servant, not a tour guide, but a competent partner who knows Nairobi and makes the client's first days easier.

What this means for you as a host

  • You are not there to impress — you are there to be useful
  • You bring local expertise that the client genuinely cannot have on day one
  • Your job is to reduce friction, not to entertain
  • You represent KIMANZI — professional, warm, and prepared
Section 2

Understanding Dutch professionals

Dutch culture has specific characteristics that directly affect how your client will behave and what they will expect from you. None of this means Dutch people are difficult — it just means they operate differently from what you might be used to.

Directness is respect, not rudeness

Dutch people are famously direct. They say what they mean, and they expect the same in return. If a client says "that restaurant wasn't great," they are not being rude — they are being honest. If you tell them "the journey will take 45 minutes," they will hold you to that. They appreciate accuracy over comfort.

✓ Do
  • Give honest estimates, even if the news isn't great
  • Say "I don't know, but I'll find out" when you're unsure
  • Be direct when something isn't possible
✗ Avoid
  • Saying what you think they want to hear
  • Vague answers like "it will be fine" or "not too far"
  • Over-promising and under-delivering

Punctuality is non-negotiable

For Dutch professionals, time is not flexible. Being on time means being slightly early. Being five minutes late without communication is already a problem. Being fifteen minutes late without a message is a serious issue.

This is especially important at the airport. A client arriving after a long flight, possibly jet-lagged, looking for their host — and finding no one — is a terrible first impression that is very hard to recover from.

Scenario — Flight delayed

Situation: Your client's flight is delayed by 90 minutes. You are already at the airport.

What to do: Send a short message as soon as you know — "Hi [name], I see your flight is delayed. I'm here and I'll wait. See you when you land." Then wait. Don't ask for updates every 20 minutes. One message is enough.

Why it matters: The client is stuck on a plane with no options. Knowing their host is calm and waiting removes one worry from their list.

Equality and informality

Dutch professionals do not expect to be treated as VIPs, and they are not comfortable with excessive formality. They will use first names immediately. They will not expect you to carry every bag or open every door — but they will appreciate it when you naturally take initiative.

Treat them as a professional peer. Warm, helpful, and competent — not subservient.

Privacy and personal space

Dutch people value privacy. They will not share personal details unless they choose to. Do not ask about their salary, relationship status, religion, or personal life. Keep conversation professional and focused on what they need.

Efficiency over ceremony

Small talk is fine but keep it brief. Dutch clients generally prefer to move efficiently — get to the accommodation, handle the practicalities, and get settled. A long ceremonial welcome can feel exhausting after an international flight. Read the room.

Section 3

Communication that works

How you communicate is just as important as what you do. KIMANZI hosts are proactive communicators — they don't wait to be asked, they anticipate and inform.

The golden rule: no surprises

Your client should never be surprised by something you could have told them in advance. Traffic, a change of plan, a delay at the SIM card shop — whatever it is, communicate it before it becomes a problem.

Proactive vs reactive communication

A reactive host waits until the client notices something is wrong. A proactive host says: "Just so you know, the road to your accommodation has some construction — it might add 15 minutes to our journey." Same situation, completely different experience for the client.

Messaging style

Keep messages short and clear. Dutch professionals receive a lot of messages — they appreciate concise communication. One topic per message. No long paragraphs. No voice notes unless specifically agreed.

✓ Good message
  • "I'm at the arrivals hall, Gate B, holding a KIMANZI sign."
  • "Your SIM is ready. Safaricom, 10GB data. KSh 1,200."
  • "Traffic on Uhuru Highway today — leaving 30 min earlier."
✗ Avoid
  • Long explanations when a short fact will do
  • Multiple messages in quick succession
  • Informal language or slang in professional context

When the client doesn't respond

If you send a message and don't hear back within a reasonable time, send one follow-up — just one. If there is still no response and it's time-sensitive, call. Do not send a string of messages. It creates anxiety on both sides.

Language

All communication with clients is in English. Your English does not need to be perfect — it needs to be clear. If you are unsure of a word, use a simple one. Simple and clear is always better than complex and confusing.

Section 4

The arrival experience

The arrival is the most important moment of the entire assignment. It sets the tone for everything that follows. A smooth arrival makes the client feel that Nairobi is manageable. A chaotic arrival makes everything harder.

Before you leave for the airport

  • Check the flight status — always. Flights from the Netherlands often come via Nairobi's JKIA but connections vary.
  • Know the client's full name and have it on a sign or your phone screen
  • Know which terminal and which arrivals gate
  • Account for Nairobi traffic — always leave earlier than you think you need to
  • Have the accommodation address saved offline, not just in Google Maps
  • Have enough cash for any unexpected costs

At the airport

Be in position before the flight lands. Not when it lands — before. Stand where you are clearly visible. Hold a sign with the client's name. Make eye contact when they emerge, smile, and greet them by name.

The first words matter. A simple "Welcome to Nairobi, [name] — I'm [your name], your KIMANZI host. Let's get you to your accommodation" is perfect. No long speech. They are tired.

The journey to accommodation

During the drive, give them a brief, useful orientation — not a tour commentary. Cover what they actually need to know:

  • Roughly how long the journey is and why (traffic, time of day)
  • The general area they are staying in
  • Anything immediately practical: "There's a supermarket two minutes from your place"
Scenario — Client is exhausted and quiet

Situation: Your client barely speaks during the journey. They look tired and give short answers.

What to do: Stop the small talk. Say something like: "You've had a long journey — I'll keep it quiet. Just let me know if you have any questions." Then drive. They will appreciate it enormously.

Why it matters: Reading the client's energy and adjusting is one of the most valued things a host can do.

At the accommodation

Help with bags. Walk them through the essentials only — door, hot water, wifi password, how to lock up. Not a full property tour unless they ask. If you are handling a SIM card and groceries on the same day, pace it — don't rush through everything at once.

End the arrival with a clear handover: "I'll check in with you tomorrow morning to see how you're settling in. If anything comes up tonight, message me." Then let them rest.

Section 5

Nairobi realities: traffic, distances, safety

One of your most important jobs is to be honest about Nairobi. Dutch professionals will use Google Maps and assume that a 7km journey takes 12 minutes. You know that is not always true. Tell them.

Traffic and distances

Nairobi's traffic is one of the most common surprises for new arrivals. A journey that looks short on a map can take 45 minutes or more during peak hours. This is not a failure — it is a reality of the city. But if you don't prepare the client for it, they will blame the delay on you.

Always brief the client on traffic upfront

Don't wait for them to be surprised. When they arrive or when planning any outing, say: "Just so you know — distances in Nairobi can be deceptive. What looks like 5km on the map can easily be 40 minutes by car during the day. I always factor this in when we plan anything."

Key things to communicate proactively

  • Peak hours in Nairobi are roughly 7:00–9:30am and 4:30–7:30pm — plan around these where possible
  • Some routes are consistently difficult (e.g. Mombasa Road during peak, Ngong Road, Waiyaki Way)
  • Bodabodas are fast but not recommended for clients — be clear about this if asked
  • Uber and Little Cab are generally reliable — help the client set these up if needed
  • Walking is not always practical or safe depending on neighbourhood and time of day

Safety

Safety is something your client will think about, even if they don't ask. Give them a realistic, calm briefing — not alarming, not dismissive.

  • Don't display phones or valuables in public, especially in busy areas
  • Stick to known routes and areas, especially at night
  • Westlands, Karen, Kilimani, Lavington are generally comfortable neighbourhoods for expats
  • Avoid walking alone after dark in unfamiliar areas
  • Matatus can be chaotic — explain this upfront if the client asks about local transport
Scenario — Client wants to walk somewhere unsafe

Situation: Your client says they want to walk from their accommodation to a meeting 2km away, at 7pm.

What to do: Don't just say "no." Explain: "That particular route at night isn't one I'd recommend — it's not well lit and it's a bit isolated. An Uber will take 5 minutes and costs about KSh 200. Want me to help you call one?" Give them an alternative, not just a refusal.

Health basics

  • Tap water in Nairobi is not recommended for drinking — always have bottled water
  • Know where the nearest pharmacy and clinic is to the client's accommodation
  • Altitude: Nairobi is at 1,795m — some clients feel slightly breathless on arrival, especially if coming from sea level. This is normal and passes within a day or two.
Section 6

Real scenarios & how to handle them

The following are realistic situations you may encounter as a KIMANZI host. Read them, think about them, and use them as a reference.

Scenario A — The client is unhappy with the accommodation

Situation: You arrive at the accommodation and the client is clearly disappointed — it doesn't match what they expected.

What to do: Stay calm and don't get defensive. Acknowledge: "I understand — let me contact KIMANZI directly so we can sort this out." Then message Stephen immediately with the specific issue. Do not make promises you cannot keep. Your job is to be the calm bridge between the client and the solution.

Scenario B — The client asks for something outside your assignment

Situation: Your client asks you to help them find a dentist, translate a legal document, or drive them to a meeting three days from now — none of which is in your confirmed assignment.

What to do: Be honest and helpful: "That's outside what I'm scheduled for, but let me check with KIMANZI — it might be something we can arrange." Don't say yes or no on your own. Always check with Stephen first for anything outside scope.

Scenario C — The client is rude or dismissive

Situation: After a stressful journey, the client is short with you or dismissive.

What to do: Don't take it personally. People under stress behave differently. Stay professional, keep doing your job well, and give them space. In most cases, once they are settled, the tone will change completely. If the behaviour is genuinely disrespectful and persistent, note it and report it to Stephen after the assignment.

Scenario D — Something goes wrong that isn't your fault

Situation: The SIM card shop is closed, the Uber driver cancels three times, the accommodation wifi isn't working.

What to do: Move to plan B without making a drama of it. "The usual shop is closed today — I know another one nearby, it'll take an extra 10 minutes." The client doesn't need to hear about all the obstacles — they need to see that you solve problems calmly. Brief Stephen after the assignment on what happened.

Scenario E — The client asks personal questions about you

Situation: A friendly client asks about your family, your income, your other work.

What to do: Answer comfortably but keep it brief and professional. You are not required to share personal details. A simple, warm deflection works: "I keep work and personal life pretty separate, but I'm happy to talk about Nairobi all day." Dutch clients will respect a clear boundary.

Section 7

The KIMANZI mindset

Beyond the practical skills, there is a way of thinking that defines a great KIMANZI host. It is not about following a checklist — it is about how you show up.

Be prepared, not reactive

A great host thinks ahead. Before every assignment, ask yourself: what could go wrong today, and what is my plan B? Traffic, a closed shop, a delayed flight, a confused client. Most problems are predictable — prepare for them.

Own your local knowledge

You know Nairobi. Your client doesn't. That is your value. Don't be shy about it. When you recommend a restaurant, a route, a shop — do it with confidence. Your expertise is exactly what the client is paying for.

Stay calm under pressure

Clients pick up on your energy. If you are calm, they feel calm. If you are flustered, they worry. Whatever is happening around you — traffic, delays, complications — your job is to be the steady presence in the situation.

You are a professional

KIMANZI hosts are not assistants or servants. You are an independent professional who happens to know Nairobi exceptionally well. Carry yourself that way. Be warm, be approachable, be helpful — and also be confident in your expertise and your boundaries.

The standard we hold ourselves to

Every client should leave their KIMANZI assignment feeling that Nairobi is more manageable than they feared — and that they had a reliable, competent person in their corner from day one. That is the KIMANZI standard. It is not complicated. It just requires showing up prepared, communicating proactively, and caring about the quality of what you deliver.

Questions, feedback, and support

You are not expected to handle everything alone. If you are unsure about something — an unusual client request, an unexpected situation, a question you can't answer — contact Stephen. That is what the host support channel is for. No question is too small.

After every assignment, a short debrief is appreciated: what went well, what was unexpected, what could be better. This is how KIMANZI improves over time — together.
